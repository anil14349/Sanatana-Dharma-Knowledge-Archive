# Voice & Identity

## Who We Sound Like

- A knowledgeable friend who has done deep research
- A museum guide who makes ancient wisdom accessible
- A researcher who respects tradition and evidence equally

## Who We Do NOT Sound Like

- A mythology fan page ("You won't BELIEVE what Shiva did!")
- A debate club ("This tradition is WRONG about...")
- A textbook ("The aforementioned deity manifestation...")
- A motivational speaker ("Unlock the SECRET power of...")
- A political page (no Hindutva vs secular framing)

## Voice Attributes

| Attribute | How It Sounds |
|-----------|---------------|
| Scholarly | "According to the Valmiki Ramayana (Sundara Kanda 1.1)..." |
| Warm | "Have you ever wondered why..." |
| Respectful | "In the Sri Vaishnava tradition..." |
| Curious | "What did the earliest sources actually say?" |
| Humble | "The historical evidence here is limited." |

# Language Rules

## General

- Clear, modern English. No unnecessary Sanskrit in body text.
- Introduce Sanskrit terms with transliteration and brief definition on first use.
- Active voice preferred.
- Carousel slide text: **40 words max per slide.**
- Captions: under 2,200 characters (Instagram limit).

## Evidence Language (Mandatory)

Every claim must use one of these hedging levels:

| Level | Template | When to Use |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Strong | "The Valmiki Ramayana describes Hanuman as..." | Direct textual evidence |
| Traditional | "Temple tradition in Karnataka holds that..." | Oral/regional tradition |
| Scholarly | "Scholars like [Name] argue that..." | Academic interpretation |
| Uncertain | "The origins of this practice remain debated." | Genuinely unclear |
| Folklore | "Popular tradition tells that... (not found in early scriptures)." | Folklore vs. scripture |

## Prohibited Language

Never use:
- "Shocking," "secret," "they don't want you to know"
- "Myth," "superstition," "primitive" (when referring to living traditions)
- "Definitely," "proven fact" (without evidence)
- "You won't believe," "This changes everything"
- Emojis in slide text (captions only, sparingly)

# Naming Conventions

## Deities (Use Standard Form)

| Standard | Variants (only in regional context) |
|----------|--------------------------------------|
| Shiva | Shankara, Mahadeva, Rudra |
| Vishnu | Narayana, Hari |
| Devi | Shakti, Amba |
| Hanuman | Anjaneya, Maruti, Bajrangbali |
| Ganesha | Ganapati, Vinayaka |
| Murugan | Kartikeya, Skanda, Subrahmanya |

## Texts

- Valmiki Ramayana (not "Ramayan" in formal content)
- Mahabharata
- Bhagavad Gita (not "Gita" alone on first reference)
- Vedas: Rigveda, Yajurveda, Samaveda, Atharvaveda

## Places

Use current official names with traditional name in parentheses on first reference: "Varanasi (Kashi)"

|-----|------|--------|

| Body text | Inter / Source Sans Pro | Regular |
| Sanskrit | Noto Sans Devanagari | Regular |

## Sizing (1080×1350 Instagram Slides)

| Element | Size |
|---------|------|
| Slide headline | 48–64 pt |
| Slide body | 28–36 pt |
| Sanskrit text | 32–40 pt |

## Rules

- Maximum 2 fonts per carousel.
- Headlines: sentence case (not ALL CAPS, except single emphasis words).
- Left-aligned or center-aligned; never justified on narrow slides.
- Consistent margins: 80px minimum on all sides.
- Bottom bar: series name or episode number (small, consistent position).

# Carousel System

## Dimensions

- 1080 × 1350px (4:5 portrait ratio)
- 3–7 slides per carousel

## Slide Archetypes (Narrative Sequence)

| Slide | Archetype | Purpose | Hook Strength |
|-------|-----------|---------|---------------|
| 1 | Hook | Stop the scroll, provoke curiosity | Highest |
| 2 | Context | Ground the reader in time/place/tradition | Medium |
| 3 | Revelation | Deliver the core insight or surprising fact | High |
| 4 | Deep Dive | Expand with detail, evidence, nuance | Medium |
| 5 | Connection | Link to related tradition, deity, or practice | Medium |
| 6 | Wonder | Emotional or aesthetic moment of awe | High |
| 7 | Call to Study | Invite deeper exploration, tease next topic | Medium |

Not every carousel needs all 7. Minimum: Hook + 2 body slides + Call to Study.

## Slide Content Rules

- **40 words max** per slide (body text, excluding headline)
- One idea per slide
- Evidence tag in notes (not always on slide face)
- Visual description for each slide (for design handoff)
- Alt text for each slide (accessibility)

# Hook Patterns (Slide 1)

Use one of these proven formulas:

## Question Hook
> "Why does Hanuman have five faces?"  
> "What does the Rigveda actually say about creation?"

## Misconception Hook
> "This isn't what the Valmiki Ramayana actually says about Lanka."  
> "Most people get this wrong about Advaita Vedanta."

## Number Hook
> "3 forms of Devi that most people confuse"  
> "5 temples that predate the 6th century"

## Visual/Object Hook
> "Look at this 12th-century Hoysala sculpture from Halebidu."  
> "This palm leaf manuscript hasn't been translated in 300 years."

## Time Hook
> "For 2,000 years, this ritual hasn't changed."  
> "Before the Puranas existed, this is what was sung."

## Contrast Hook
> "The North worships him as a warrior. The South sees a philosopher."  
> "The popular story vs. what the earliest text says."

## Bold Statement Hook
> "Kashi is not just a city. It's a cosmological diagram."  
> "The Vedas never mention this practice."

# Series & Curriculum Levels

## Curriculum Levels

| Level | Audience | Depth | Language |
|-------|----------|-------|----------|
| 1–3 | Curious beginner | Overview, storytelling, "did you know" | Simple, no assumed knowledge |
| 4–6 | Engaged learner | Connections, comparisons, regional depth | Some Sanskrit, assumes basics |
| 7–9 | Serious student | Textual analysis, scholarly debate, rare manuscripts | Technical, assumes familiarity |

## Series Types

| Series Type | Description | Example |
|-------------|-------------|---------|
| Deity Deep Dive | Multi-part exploration of one deity | "Hanuman: Beyond the Obvious" (5 parts) |
| Temple Trail | Architecture + history of a temple group | "Hoysala Temples" (4 parts) |
| Text Explorer | One scripture, broken into themes | "Inside the Bhagavad Gita" (7 parts) |
| Concept Series | One philosophical idea explored fully | "What is Dharma?" (4 parts) |
| Festival Focus | Deep context behind a celebration | "Navaratri: 9 Nights Decoded" (3 parts) |
| Regional | Specific geographic tradition | "Kerala's Living Sanskrit" (4 parts) |
| Myth vs. Text | Popular story vs. earliest source | "What Valmiki Actually Wrote" (5 parts) |

Each series should have 3–7 episodes. Number them: "Episode 1/5" in bottom bar.

# Posting Rhythm

## Recommended Weekly Schedule

| Day | Format | Content Type |
|-----|--------|-------------|
| Mon | Carousel | Educational (Level 1–6) |
| Wed | Reel | Hook/Storytelling (30-45 sec) |
| Fri | Carousel OR Quote Post | Series episode OR wisdom post |

Minimum: 3 posts/week. Maximum: 5 (avoid audience fatigue).

## Seasonal Calendar

Plan content around:
- **Navaratri** (Sep/Oct) — Devi series
- **Diwali** (Oct/Nov) — Lakshmi, Rama, light symbolism
- **Maha Shivaratri** (Feb/Mar) — Shiva deep dives
- **Ram Navami** (Mar/Apr) — Ramayana series
- **Guru Purnima** (Jul) — Lineage and parampara
- **Ganesh Chaturthi** (Aug/Sep) — Ganesha series
- **Pongal/Makar Sankranti** (Jan) — Solar traditions, regional

Start seasonal content 2 weeks before the festival.

# AI Usage Rules (For ChatGPT Content Generation)

## Mandatory Guardrails

1. **Never invent scripture citations.** Use only what is provided in the Knowledge Object or verified sources. If you don't have a source, write `[NEEDS VERIFICATION]`.

2. **Never generate Sanskrit etymology** unless the source material provides it. Wrong etymology is worse than no etymology.

3. **Never make up temple histories, dates, or architectural claims.** If unsure, write `[CHECK SOURCE]`.

4. **Never present interpretation as fact.** Use evidence language (see Evidence Language section above).

5. **Never use "myth" or "mythology"** to describe living traditions. Use: tradition, narrative, sacred history, scripture, itihasa.

6. **Never add emojis to slide text.** Emojis are only acceptable in captions, and only sparingly (max 3 per caption).

7. **Never hallucinate verse numbers.** "Bhagavad Gita 4.7" must be real. If you're not certain, omit the verse number.

8. **Flag uncertainty explicitly.** If a claim feels thin, mark it `[LOW CONFIDENCE]` for human review.

9. **Don't invent visual descriptions of deities** beyond what iconographic tradition specifies. A wrong number of arms or wrong attribute in hand is disrespectful.

10. **Respect tradition boundaries.** Don't mix Shaiva and Vaishnava narratives carelessly. Don't present one sampradaya's view as universal.

## Output Format Rules

- Always output in the carousel template structure (slide-by-slide)
- Include alt text for every slide
- Include the caption separately after slides
- Mark any claim that needs verification
- State which Knowledge Object the content is derived from

# Example: Completed Carousel

## Carousel Plan: Panchamukha Hanuman — The Five-Faced Form

**KO Reference:** TOP-0001  
**Series:** Hanuman: Beyond the Obvious — Episode 3/5  
**Curriculum Level:** 4  
**Dominant Color:** Saffron + Deep Indigo  
**Art Style:** Classical South Indian temple sculpture photography

---

### Slide 1 — Hook

**Archetype:** Hook  
**Headline:** Why does Hanuman have five faces?  
**Body:** The answer isn't in the Ramayana. It comes from a battle that most people have never heard of.  
**Visual:** Close-up of Panchamukha Hanuman sculpture (stone, South Indian style), dramatic lighting  
**Alt text:** Stone sculpture of five-faced Hanuman from a South Indian temple, showing all five animal and deity faces in profile

---

### Slide 2 — Context

**Archetype:** Context  
**Headline:** The story behind the form  
**Body:** In the Ramayana war, Ahiravana drags Rama and Lakshmana to Patala. To rescue them, Hanuman must extinguish five lamps simultaneously — one in each direction.  
**Visual:** Illustrated map showing Patala (underworld) with five directional lamps  
**Alt text:** Illustrated diagram showing five oil lamps placed in cardinal directions plus center, representing the challenge Hanuman faced in Patala

---

### Slide 3 — Revelation

**Archetype:** Revelation  
**Headline:** Five faces, five directions  
**Body:** Hanuman assumes five faces to blow out all five lamps at once: Hanuman (East), Narasimha (South), Garuda (West), Varaha (North), Hayagriva (Upward).  
**Evidence tag:** Parasara Samhita; regional Ramayana traditions (not in Valmiki Ramayana)  
**Visual:** Labeled diagram of the five faces with directional arrows  
**Alt text:** Diagram showing five faces of Panchamukha Hanuman labeled with deity names and cardinal directions

---

### Slide 4 — Deep Dive

**Archetype:** Deep Dive  
**Headline:** Each face carries meaning  
**Body:** Narasimha destroys fear. Garuda removes poison and black magic. Varaha grants prosperity. Hayagriva bestows knowledge. Hanuman himself grants courage.  
**Visual:** Five-panel grid, each showing one face with its attribute  
**Alt text:** Five-panel grid showing each face of Panchamukha Hanuman with their associated spiritual quality

---

### Slide 5 — Connection

**Archetype:** Connection  
**Headline:** A Vaishnavite theology in one form  
**Body:** All five faces are Vishnu's avataras or servants. This form encodes the idea that Hanuman contains the full power of Vishnu's protective manifestations.  
**Related topics:** Narasimha, Varaha, Dashavatara  
**Visual:** Subtle diagram connecting all five to Vishnu at center  
**Alt text:** Diagram showing all five faces connected to central Vishnu, illustrating theological relationship

---

### Slide 6 — Call to Study

**Archetype:** Call to Study  
**Headline:** Next: Where you can see this form  
**Body:** The most powerful Panchamukha Hanuman temples — and why each region sculpts him differently.  
**Visual:** Teaser collage of 3 different Panchamukha sculptures from different regions  
**Alt text:** Three different regional sculptures of five-faced Hanuman showing stylistic differences between North, South, and Eastern Indian traditions

---

### Caption

Why does Hanuman have five faces?

The Panchamukha form doesn't appear in the Valmiki Ramayana. It emerges from a specific episode — the rescue of Rama from Patala — found in the Parasara Samhita and regional Ramayana traditions.

Each face represents a direction, a Vaishnavite deity, and a spiritual power. Together they encode a complete theology of protection.

This is Episode 3 in our "Hanuman: Beyond the Obvious" series. Next: the temples where this form is worshipped — and how each region sculpts him differently.
