## 1. Introduction & Philosophy

### 1.1 Core Research Mandate

The research function of SDKA serves a single primary objective:

**To discover, verify, organize, and present authentic knowledge of Sanātana Dharma and Indic Knowledge Systems.**

This mandate is governed by a single hierarchy of values:

```
Research First
    ↓
Understanding Second
    ↓
Storytelling Third
    ↓
Visual Excellence Fourth
    ↓
Virality Last
```

**Critical principle:** Virality is welcomed only when authenticity is preserved. Never sacrifice truth for engagement.

### 1.2 Research vs. Scholarship

**Research at SDKA:**
- Begins with questions rather than conclusions
- Uses evidence to test assumptions
- Accepts uncertainty when appropriate
- Presents multiple authentic viewpoints
- Traces claims to source material
- Respects both traditional knowledge and historical scholarship

**What we are NOT:**
- A mythology page (we are a research institution)
- A popular blog (we are academic-adjacent but accessible)
- An ideology promotion vehicle (we present evidence, not belief)
- A complete reference (we acknowledge knowledge gaps)

## 2. Research Principles

### 2.1 Authenticity Principle

Every research effort must use the **strongest available traditional and historical evidence**.

**Guidance:**
- Primary sources over secondary interpretations
- Multiple witnesses over single attestations
- Ancient manuscripts over modern retellings
- Scriptural basis over folk variations (unless documenting folk variations specifically)
- Direct quotations over paraphrases

**When evidence is missing:**
- Say so explicitly
- Mark speculation as "tradition holds that..." or "scholars believe..."
- Distinguish between historical fact, textual assertion, and community belief

### 2.2 Intellectual Honesty Principle

Truth is more valuable than consistency.

**Guidance:**
- If two authoritative traditions disagree, present both viewpoints
- If historical evidence contradicts popular belief, explain both
- If a topic is contested in scholarship, show different scholarly positions
- If evidence is uncertain, label it as uncertain
- If knowledge is incomplete, acknowledge the gap

**Example structure:**
> The mainstream tradition holds that [X]. However, [Y] region practices [Z], based on [source]. Scholars debate whether this represents [interpretation A] or [interpretation B].

### 2.3 Respect Principle

The archive respects:
- Scripture and textual traditions
- Living communities and their practices
- Temple sanctity and ritual integrity
- Guru-paramparā (teacher lineages) and their authority
- Regional and tribal traditions
- Different schools of thought

**Never ridicule sincerely held beliefs.** Explain them respectfully, even when critiquing specific claims.

### 2.4 Curiosity Principle

Every piece of research should satisfy or create intellectual curiosity.

**Guidance:**
- Ask questions that most people haven't thought to ask
- Move beyond surface-level facts to deeper understanding
- Connect knowledge to living practice and historical evolution
- Explain the "why" behind customs, not just the "what"

### 2.5 Preservation Principle

Documentation exists to capture knowledge before it disappears.

**Guidance:**
- Prioritize researching:
  - Rare and endangered texts
  - Oral traditions not yet written down
  - Regional customs that are changing
  - Manuscripts in poor condition
  - Knowledge held by aging practitioners
  - Tribal and folk traditions at risk
- Explain how knowledge functions in context, not as isolated fact

## 3. Research Workflow

### 3.1 Phase 1: Research Planning

**Objective:** Define the research project clearly before beginning.

**Steps:**

1. **Identify the Question**
   - What exactly are we trying to understand?
   - Why is this knowledge important?
   - Who would benefit from this knowledge?
   - Does this fit SDKA's scope?

2. **Define Scope**
   - Geographic scope (which regions/traditions?)
   - Temporal scope (which time periods?)
   - Textual scope (which scriptures/sources?)
   - Audience scope (what level of expertise required?)

3. **Preliminary Source Assessment**
   - What sources are available?
   - What languages do they exist in?
   - What interpretive traditions exist?
   - What gaps exist?

4. **Determine Research Type**
   - Textual analysis (examining scriptures)
   - Comparative study (multiple traditions)
   - Historical investigation (chronology, evidence)
   - Regional documentation (local practice)
   - Living tradition study (contemporary practice)
   - Manuscript research (rare or damaged texts)

**Deliverable:** Research plan document (1-2 pages)

### 3.2 Phase 2: Source Collection & Verification

**Objective:** Gather reliable sources and verify their credibility.

**Steps:**

1. **Identify Primary Sources**
   - Original texts (Vedas, Puranas, Agamas, etc.)
   - Manuscripts and inscriptions
   - Historical records
   - Eyewitness accounts
   - Contemporary practice documentation

2. **Identify Secondary Sources**
   - Scholarly works
   - Academic commentary
   - Interpretive traditions
   - Historical analyses
   - Expert interviews

3. **Assess Source Reliability**
   - Is the source from a credible tradition or scholar?
   - Is there internal consistency?
   - Are claims supported by evidence?
   - What is the author's expertise and potential bias?
   - Has this source been peer-reviewed or endorsed by tradition?

4. **Track All Sources**
   - Create full citations for every source
   - Note page numbers for specific quotes
   - Record access dates for online sources
   - Document any translations used

**Deliverable:** Annotated source list with reliability assessments

### 3.3 Phase 3: Analysis & Synthesis

**Objective:** Extract understanding from sources and synthesize into coherent knowledge.

**Steps:**

1. **Close Reading of Sources**
   - What does the source actually say (not what we think it says)?
   - What is the context of the statement?
   - Are there alternate interpretations?
   - What assumptions does the source make?

2. **Cross-Reference and Compare**
   - Do multiple sources agree?
   - Where do they disagree?
   - Are disagreements due to perspective or factual difference?
   - Can disagreements be reconciled?

3. **Identify Patterns and Connections**
   - What themes appear across sources?
   - How does this knowledge connect to related topics?
   - What evolution or development is evident?
   - What remain unanswered questions?

4. **Document Uncertainty**
   - Where is evidence clear vs. ambiguous?
   - Where do scholars disagree?
   - Where do traditions differ?
   - Where is knowledge incomplete?

**Deliverable:** Research notes with analysis, synthesis, and noted uncertainties

### 3.4 Phase 4: Knowledge Object Creation

**Objective:** Organize research into reusable knowledge objects.

**Steps:**

1. **Structure the Knowledge Object**
   - Use the Knowledge Object Template (see section 5)
   - Organize information hierarchically
   - Provide clear definitions upfront
   - Build complexity gradually

2. **Write for the Archive**
   - Write for reference, not narrative
   - Use clear, precise language
   - Define specialized terms
   - Include cross-references to related topics

3. **Add Source Attribution**
   - Cite all factual claims
   - Link to primary sources where accessible
   - Note translation versions used
   - Acknowledge interpretive traditions

4. **Create Derivative Content**
   - Identify what can become social media content
   - Note potential visual approaches
   - Suggest story angles for narrative content
   - Flag interesting research questions for video scripts

**Deliverable:** Complete knowledge object with internal documentation

### 3.5 Phase 5: Quality Review & Finalization

**Objective:** Ensure accuracy, clarity, and consistency before publishing.

**Steps:**

1. **Accuracy Review**
   - Verify all citations are correct
   - Check quote accuracy
   - Confirm interpretations match sources
   - Validate any translations

2. **Clarity Review**
   - Can someone unfamiliar with the topic understand it?
   - Are technical terms defined?
   - Is the structure logical?
   - Are examples helpful?

3. **Consistency Review**
   - Does tone match style guide?
   - Are definitions consistent with other entries?
   - Do cross-references work?
   - Is metadata complete?

4. **Editorial Sign-Off**
   - Editor reviews for quality
   - Subject matter expert reviews for accuracy (if external to researcher)
   - Final corrections incorporated

**Deliverable:** Finalized, approved knowledge object

## 4. Source Evaluation Framework

### 4.1 Source Hierarchy (for this project)

1. **Tier 1: Foundational Scriptures (Highest Authority)**
   - Vedas (Rigveda, Yajurveda, Samaveda, Atharvaveda)
   - Upanishads (108 principle and subsidiary)
   - Brahmasutra and related philosophy
   - Approved Smritis and Shastras

2. **Tier 2: Primary Religious & Textual Sources**
   - Puranas
   - Itihasas (Ramayana, Mahabharata)
   - Agamas
   - Authentic Tantras
   - Commentaries by recognized Acharyas

3. **Tier 3: Historical & Regional Sources**
   - Inscriptions and epigraphs
   - Manuscripts and manuscripts collections
   - Historical records
   - Regional texts and traditions
   - Temple records

4. **Tier 4: Scholarly & Contemporary Sources**
   - Academic scholarship (peer-reviewed)
   - Expert practitioner documentation
   - Historical research
   - Living tradition studies

5. **Tier 5: Secondary & Interpretive**
   - Popular interpretations
   - Modern spiritual literature
   - Online databases
   - Popular media

### 4.2 Evaluating a Specific Source

Ask these questions:

1. **Authority & Expertise**
   - Who is the author or source?
   - What is their expertise?
   - Is this their field of specialization?
   - Are they recognized by relevant communities?

2. **Methodology**
   - How did they arrive at this knowledge?
   - Did they cite their sources?
   - Is their methodology sound?
   - Are they transparent about assumptions?

3. **Evidence**
   - Do they support claims with evidence?
   - Are citations provided?
   - Can the evidence be verified?
   - Is evidence primary or secondary?

4. **Bias**
   - What is the author's perspective?
   - Do they acknowledge limitations?
   - Are they sponsored or influenced by outside interests?
   - Does their conclusion align with evidence, or does evidence fit conclusion?

5. **Peer Review**
   - Has this been reviewed by experts?
   - Is it published in a reputable venue?
   - Has it been critiqued or supported by scholarship?
   - Do multiple sources corroborate?

### 4.3 The Red Flags

Be skeptical of:
- Sources with no citations
- Claims that contradict multiple authoritative sources without explanation
- Anonymous sources with no verifiable expertise
- Sources that claim certainty in ambiguous matters
- Sources that ridicule or demean entire traditions
- Sources that require buying additional materials to verify
- Popular media presenting themselves as scholarly

## 5. Knowledge Object Creation

### 5.1 Knowledge Object Structure

Every research project should be converted into a reusable **Knowledge Object**.

**A Knowledge Object is:**
- A self-contained unit of knowledge
- Organized hierarchically (general to specific)
- Well-sourced and verifiable
- Connected to related objects
- Suitable for repurposing into various content formats

### 5.2 Knowledge Object Template

Use the template in `/templates/knowledge_object_template.md`

**Required sections:**

1. **Title & Metadata**
   - Topic name
   - Category/ies
   - Related topics
   - Research status (draft/verified/final)
   - Research date

2. **Summary**
   - 2-3 sentence overview
   - Key claim or central idea
   - Why this matters

3. **Context**
   - Historical or cultural context
   - Geographic scope
   - Temporal span
   - Relevant traditions

4. **Core Content**
   - Main information organized hierarchically
   - Clear definitions upfront
   - Building complexity gradually
   - Worked examples where helpful

5. **Evidence & Sources**
   - Primary source citations
   - Translation versions noted
   - Scholarly references
   - Expert sources

6. **Disagreements & Alternatives**
   - Where do traditions differ?
   - What do scholars debate?
   - Alternative interpretations noted
   - Why differences exist

7. **Related Knowledge**
   - Cross-references to connected topics
   - "See also" suggestions
   - Hierarchical relationships
   - Network connections

8. **Gaps & Unknowns**
   - What questions remain unanswered?
   - What information is incomplete?
   - What would further research reveal?
   - What sources are unavailable?

9. **Derivative Content Notes**
   - Potential social media angles
   - Visual treatment suggestions
   - Story opportunities
   - Teaching applications

### 5.3 Writing Standards for Knowledge Objects

**Tone:**
- Authoritative but accessible
- Scholarly but not pedantic
- Respectful to traditions
- Clear to educated general audience

**Structure:**
- Start with overview
- Proceed from general to specific
- Use hierarchy (sections, subsections)
- Define specialized terms
- Use examples to clarify

**Evidence:**
- Cite all factual claims
- Use numbered or lettered citations
- Distinguish fact from interpretation
- Label speculation explicitly

**Accuracy:**
- Verify all quotes
- Use original language where important
- Note translation versions
- Acknowledge ambiguities

## 7. Common Research Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Researching a Deity

**Research flow:**
1. Identify all authoritative texts mentioning the deity
2. Trace evolution across textual traditions
3. Map regional variations and local practices
4. Document iconography and symbolism
5. Connect to philosophical significance
6. Note festival associations
7. Document temple presence and worship

**Key sources:**
- Vedic references (if applicable)
- Puranic accounts
- Regional temple traditions
- Living practice documentation
- Scholarly works on comparative mythology

**Complexity factors:**
- Deity may have multiple forms/manifestations
- Traditions may disagree on origins
- Regional practices may vary significantly
- Popular practice may diverge from textual basis

**Example output:** Complete deity knowledge object with myth, practice, symbolism, and regional variations

### Scenario 2: Researching a Ritual Practice

**Research flow:**
1. Find textual basis (Agama, Vidhi, etc.)
2. Document variations by tradition/region
3. Explain symbolic meaning
4. Trace historical evolution
5. Connect to larger ritual system
6. Document contemporary practice
7. Note any contested interpretations

**Key sources:**
- Agama texts
- Smriti literature
- Temple protocols and records
- Practitioner interviews
- Regional manuscripts

**Complexity factors:**
- Practices evolve over time
- Regional variations are significant
- Practitioners may disagree on correct form
- Textual basis may be implicit rather than explicit

**Example output:** Ritual knowledge object with textual basis, variations by region, symbolic meaning, and current practice

### Scenario 3: Researching a Temple

**Research flow:**
1. Research founding and historical development
2. Document architectural features
3. Identify presiding deity and tradition
4. Document main rituals and calendar
5. Research regional and scriptural significance
6. Document recent history and current status
7. Connect to broader pilgrimage networks

**Key sources:**
- Temple records and inscriptions
- Historical chronicles
- Architectural studies
- Pilgrimage literature
- Academic works on temple traditions

**Complexity factors:**
- Temples often have complex founding histories
- Architectural styles may change over centuries
- Ritual practices may have evolved
- Current practice may differ from historical practice

**Example output:** Temple knowledge object with history, architecture, deity, rituals, and significance

### Scenario 4: Researching a Textual Topic

**Research flow:**
1. Identify all authoritative texts addressing topic
2. Compare how different texts treat topic
3. Trace evolution across textual traditions
4. Document philosophical interpretations
5. Connect to contemporary practice
6. Identify areas of scholarly debate
7. Note any lost or rare source materials

**Key sources:**
- Primary scriptures (Vedas, Puranas, etc.)
- Commentarial traditions
- Related philosophical works
- Scholarly studies
- Practitioner interpretations

**Complexity factors:**
- Topic may be interpreted differently by schools
- Textual evidence may be ambiguous
- Different philosophical traditions may disagree
- Modern scholarship may offer new perspectives

**Example output:** Comprehensive knowledge object with textual basis, interpretive traditions, philosophical implications, and practical applications

## 8. Managing Disagreement & Multiple Viewpoints

### 8.1 When Traditions Disagree

The archive's approach to disagreement:

1. **Acknowledge both viewpoints**
   - Present each viewpoint clearly
   - Explain the basis for each interpretation
   - Note which sources support each view

2. **Show why they disagree**
   - Is it textual interpretation?
   - Is it based on different sources?
   - Is it theological vs. practical?
   - Is it regional variation?

3. **Avoid forced reconciliation**
   - Don't try to merge disagreements that genuinely exist
   - Don't privilege one tradition over another without justification
   - Don't present uncertainty as certainty

4. **Let readers decide**
   - Provide enough information for informed judgment
   - Acknowledge that different traditions may be equally valid
   - Note if one viewpoint is more historically documented

### 8.2 When Scholars Disagree

For scholarly disagreements:

1. **Present multiple positions**
   - What does scholar A argue?
   - What does scholar B argue?
   - What evidence supports each?

2. **Explain the disagreement**
   - Is it based on different evidence?
   - Different interpretive frameworks?
   - Different methodological assumptions?

3. **Note scholarly consensus (if it exists)**
   - What do most scholars agree on?
   - What remains genuinely contested?

4. **Acknowledge limitations**
   - No scholar has perfect knowledge
   - Scholarship evolves
   - New evidence may change conclusions

### 8.3 When Evidence is Unclear

For ambiguous or incomplete evidence:

**Use precise language:**
- "The earliest sources suggest..."
- "Tradition holds that..."
- "Scholars debate whether..."
- "There is no clear evidence that..."
- "Archaeological evidence has not yet been found for..."
- "Regional tradition reports that..."

**Always acknowledge:**
- What is known with confidence
- What is probable but not certain
- What remains unknown
- Where more research is needed

## 9. Documentation Standards

### 9.1 Citation Format

For knowledge objects, use clear in-text citations:

**For direct quotes:**
> "Direct quotation here" (Source Name, Section/Page, Translation Note if relevant)

**For paraphrases:**
According to [Source Name], the concept of [X] originated in [Y]. (Source Name, Section/Page)

**For references at end of document:**
Create a Sources section with:
- Full title of work
- Author/Tradition if applicable
- Edition or translation used
- Sections referenced
- Access information for online sources

### 9.2 Cross-References

Link related knowledge objects:

**Example structure:**
- See also: [Related Topic 1], [Related Topic 2]
- Part of larger topic: [Parent Topic]
- Contains sub-topics: [Sub-topic 1], [Sub-topic 2]
- Contrasts with: [Alternative Concept]
- Supports: [Related Practice/Philosophy]

### 9.3 Metadata

Every research project should include:

```
Topic: [Name of topic]
Categories: [Primary category], [Secondary categories]
Research Status: [Draft/In Review/Verified/Final]
Researcher: [Name/Team]
Date Created: [Date]
Last Updated: [Date]
Related Topics: [List]
Primary Sources: [Key source count]
Complexity Level: [Beginner/Intermediate/Advanced]
```

## 11. Research Ethics & Responsibilities

### 11.1 Respect for Living Traditions

When researching active traditions:

- Interview practitioners respectfully
- Don't publish information communities wish to keep private
- Acknowledge community expertise
- Credit living teachers and scholars
- Respect sacred practices—describe them appropriately but not for spectacle

### 11.2 Source Respect

- Don't misquote sources
- Don't take statements out of context
- Acknowledge limitations of translations
- Don't claim to have read primary sources you haven't
- Verify information before publishing

### 11.3 Intellectual Honesty

- Credit sources of ideas
- Don't present others' research as your own
- Acknowledge when changing previous positions
- Document what you don't know
- Pursue truth over ego
